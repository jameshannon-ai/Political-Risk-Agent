## 1. Executive Judgement

The Strait of Hormuz remains a severe marine insurance exposure because disruption is expressed through connected channels: safe transit uncertainty, carrier caution, oil and LNG cargo concentration, war-risk repricing, trapped vessel exposure and sanctions/compliance complexity.

For a marine insurer, the central issue is accumulation. Hull war, cargo war, liability, reinsurance appetite and policy wording can all be affected by the same transit shock. The underwriting stance should remain conservative over the next 1-3 months, with controls relaxed only when practical operating evidence confirms normalisation.

De-escalation evidence should affect monitoring posture before underwriting controls. The practical tests are vessel-flow recovery, carrier willingness to transit, broker/reinsurance pricing and updated official maritime guidance.

## 4. Evidence-Led Risk Drivers

### 1. Carrier and operational disruption
**Evidence signal:** Maersk said operating conditions around the Strait of Hormuz remained constrained, shipping traffic continued well below normal levels, safe transit could not be assured, and no changes were being made to specific services.

**Quantified detail:** 4

**Highest-weight sources:** HORMUZ-002

**Why it matters commercially:** Operator risk appetite and practical service constraints.

**Client implication:** Trapped vessel, delay, deviation and cargo accumulation risk.

**Decision use:** Informs whether transit controls should remain enhanced.

**Refresh trigger:** Refresh before changing referral rules or after major carrier service updates.

### 2. De-escalation and stabilisation evidence
**Evidence signal:** Axios reported that a possible U.S.-Iran deal could reopen the Strait of Hormuz while nuclear talks proceed, but analysts cautioned that normalisation could be stop-start and depend on vessel-owner and crew confidence, mine clearance, insurance rates, and operating conditions.

**Quantified detail:** No quantified detail extracted.

**Highest-weight sources:** HORMUZ-008

**Why it matters commercially:** Credible stabilising evidence defines conditions under which disruption may ease.

**Client implication:** Prevents excessive downside bias while preserving controls.

**Decision use:** Informs relaxation triggers without automatically reducing controls.

**Refresh trigger:** Refresh before relaxing controls; verify against operational and pricing evidence.

### 3. Energy chokepoint exposure
**Evidence signal:** IEA says the Strait of Hormuz handled about 20 mb/d of crude oil and oil products in 2025, around 25% of world seaborne oil trade, and that about 93% of Qatar's LNG exports and 96% of UAE LNG exports transit the Strait.

**Quantified detail:** 20, 20 mb/d, 2025, 25%, 93%, 96%

**Highest-weight sources:** HORMUZ-003

**Why it matters commercially:** Structural exposure across oil, LNG, tanker demand and cargo values.

**Client implication:** Severe impact rating and accumulation stress testing.

**Decision use:** Supports severe impact scoring even where transit partially resumes.

**Refresh trigger:** Refresh when structural oil/LNG flow assumptions or chokepoint data are updated.

### 4. Insurance-market repricing
**Evidence signal:** Howden Re reported war-risk pricing for Hormuz transit rising from roughly 0.10-0.125% of vessel value pre-conflict to 2-3% in March 2026, with worst-case voyage premiums for a $100M vessel estimated at roughly $375,000-$3M per transit.

**Quantified detail:** 0.10, 0.125%, 2, 3%, 3, 2026

**Highest-weight sources:** HORMUZ-004

**Why it matters commercially:** Pricing and reinsurance markets have repriced route risk.

**Client implication:** Premium adequacy, limits, deductibles, reinsurance appetite and referral thresholds.

**Decision use:** Supports pricing review and capacity check.

**Refresh trigger:** Refresh before binding Gulf-linked risk or after broker/reinsurance market movement.

### 5. Maritime security and transit risk
**Evidence signal:** IMO said there was no safe transit anywhere in the Strait of Hormuz, citing verified vessel attacks, detained vessels, mines risk, and around 20,000 seafarers trapped on about 1,600 vessels in the Gulf.

**Quantified detail:** 20, 000 seafarers, 000, 1, 600, 600 vessels

**Highest-weight sources:** HORMUZ-001

**Why it matters commercially:** Safe passage uncertainty, crew welfare, route approval and live transit risk.

**Client implication:** Hull war controls, liability exposure, claims preparedness and voyage approval.

**Decision use:** Supports enhanced referral and route-level controls.

**Refresh trigger:** Refresh before route controls are relaxed or if new incident advisories are issued.

### 6. Vessel-flow and market behaviour

## 7. Recommended Underwriting Actions

1. Accumulation review: refresh route-level exposure across hull war, cargo war, assured, vessel class, cargo type and Gulf transit dependency.
2. Pricing review: benchmark premium adequacy against current broker and reinsurance indicators before binding new Gulf-linked risks.
3. Referral controls: tighten referral rules for Gulf transit, tanker cargoes, unclear counterparties and sanctions-sensitive cargoes.
4. Wording review: check war, strikes, blocking/trapping, sanctions, notice and cancellation provisions.
5. Reinsurance check: confirm appetite, exclusions, attachment points and any changes in available capacity.
6. Claims preparedness: review trapped vessel, blocked passage, cargo delay and crew welfare response assumptions.
7. Relaxation test: ease controls only after vessel-flow recovery, carrier confidence and war-risk premium movement support normalisation.
